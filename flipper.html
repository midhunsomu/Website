A versatile gadget for wireless antics

What is it?

The Flipper Zero looks like a toy but is actually a powerful multi-tool for hacking. It's a small, plastic device with a playful dolphin on its screen. Despite its cute appearance, it's a versatile tool, similar to a Swiss Army knife, for interacting with electronics. Successfully funded on Kickstarter with $4.6 million, it's open-source and stylish.

What makes the Flipper Zero unique is its flexibility. Unlike other tools, it can communicate with various devices, including sub-1GHz devices (like old garage doors), Low- and High-Frequency RFID, NFC cards, Infrared devices, and Bluetooth. While some use it for pranks, like remotely opening Tesla charging ports, its true strength is in its ability to interact with nearly any wireless device.


what can it do?

The Flipper Zero is a versatile tool with multiple antennas for various tasks. The sub-1GHz transceiver interacts with devices like old garage doors, pagers, gates, gas station price signs, and doorbells. The 125kHz antenna reads, clones, and emulates older prox cards. The NFC module reads, writes, and emulates both low and high-frequency NFC devices like tap cards. The infrared transceiver learns and replicates IR device signals, like remotes for air conditioners or sound bars. Additionally, the Flipper can perform BadUSB attacks when connected to a computer via USB, using Ducky Scripts. It can also store U2F keys for two-factor authentication. You can control it with your phone via Bluetooth and store data on a microSD card.

Importantly, the Flipper Zero does not have WiFi out of the box. However, you can easily add a WiFi Devboard or ESP8266 using the GPIO pins for projects like pen-testing, deauth, probes, and more. The device benefits from a robust and vibrant community, with numerous online resources and creative uses constantly being discovered. It can run a unique version of DOOM, as well as Tetris and Flappy Bird. You can emulate Skylanders and Amiibos, and even engage in old-fashioned phone phreaking with a working pay phone. Being open source, you can also install your own firmware, which many users do.


How much of a threat is it?

This is where we get into territory that gets shipments held up by Customs. The Flipper Zero is a powerful tool and could be used maliciously in the wrong hands, but this is also true for a Raspberry Pi turned into a Pwnagotchi, various ESP8266 boards, or even just a phone.

It's important to remember that a tool's usefulness or danger depends on the user. The tools to create chaos exist, but it takes planning and intent. An individual has to choose to copy keycards, tamper with gas station price signs, or force someone's computer to subscribe to a YouTube channel. Most hacking tools are primarily useful for testing one's own security, not attacking others.

Some people just want to experiment with wireless signals. Many Flipper Zero users simply use it for harmless tasks like turning on their air conditioner. On its own, the Flipper Zero isn't going to turn IT professionals into hackers from a video game. Many users will just use it to clone keys for convenience or play harmless pranks on Tesla owners.

Could I use it myself?

Absolutely. The app for the Flipper Zero is very straightforward, with a simple interface. Users continuously add robust scripts, easily found on GitHub. There's also a healthy forum and a friendly Discord server for help with projects, which is quite rare these days.

That is, of course, if you can get your hands on one. The same goes for a Raspberry Pi. Good luck with that in the near future!
