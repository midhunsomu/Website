Wireless card readers are common, but they can be easily spoofed or attacked with the right tools.

What is it?

The ChameleonMini is a tool for emulating and cloning high-frequency contactless cards and reading RFID tags. It works as an NFC emulator and RFID reader and can sniff and log radio frequency (RF) data. Visually, it resembles a credit card but comes in various forms. You can use it standalone or connect it to your phone via Bluetooth and use chameleon apps for penetration testing on your systems.

If you have an employeeâ€™s key fob, the ChameleonMini can create a functional replica to access any area the original fob would. It also has more advanced capabilities we'll discuss later.

The device originated as an open-source project on GitHub in 2013, leading to multiple versions. The Revision G, successfully kickstarted by KAOS in 2016, is a popular version. It's powered by a rechargeable battery and comes in several colors.

Proxmark offers two miniature models: the Chameleon Tiny and the Chameleon Tiny Pro. The Chameleon Tiny is small enough to fit on a keychain, while the Chameleon Tiny Pro features Bluetooth Low Energy for quick communication with Android and iOS apps.


what can it do?

The ChameleonMini can extract information from card keys and key fobs, including cloning the UID and storing the data for later use. While card keys are its primary target, the Chameleon can also execute an MFKey32 attack to target RFID readers. Additionally, it can sniff for keys and crack them, although you need to be fairly close for this to work.

However, the ChameleonMini does not work on low-frequency RFID cards like the Proxmark3 and Flipper Zero can. If you need to cover low-frequency cards, many affordable devices are available online for that purpose.


How much of a threat is it?

If your security system uses RF signals, this kind of attack is a significant threat. The ChameleonMini, while less powerful than devices like the Proxmark3 (the most popular) or the ICopy-X (built on the Proxmark3), is simpler to use, inconspicuous, and can be used alongside these tools for more efficient hacking. It can even clone amiibos, though somewhat inconsistently.

There are simpler tools available on Amazon for less than $30 that can be surprisingly effective. Many legacy keycard systems haven't been updated due to laziness or ignorance, making them vulnerable to even basic tools. Sometimes, the simplest tool is the most effective in hacking.

RFID projects like the ChameleonMini and Proxmark3 have been around for a while, with substantial open-source support, thanks to contributors like the prolific Iceman. The ChameleonMini supports emulation of multiple NFC chipsets, including a wide array of Mifare cards and codecs. It can execute an MFKey32 attack and perform limited sniffing, cracking, and logging.

Could I use it myself?

If you're handy, then probably yes. There are multiple apps for the Chameleon family of devices, such as the one by the RFID Research Group, that allow you to control the device from your phone. Additionally, there are good online tutorials, including a robust crash course on GitHub. Among hacking devices, the Chameleon is one of the more approachable options for newbies and aspiring hackers.

However, using the Chameleon isn't as simple as waving it at a door to magically open it. It requires a decent amount of know-how and strategy, so be prepared to study different contactless and proximity card standards. This complexity makes it an excellent tool for learning about security, enabling you to conduct penetration tests and identify flaws in your systems.
